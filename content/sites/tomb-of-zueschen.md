## Architecture and Features

The tomb of Züschen is characterized as a dolmen, a term that refers to a specific type of megalithic structure. Typically, dolmens consist of several large stones arranged to form a chamber, often covered by a capstone. The architectural style is indicative of the Neolithic period, when communal burial practices were prevalent.

Visitors to the Züschen site will observe large, upright stones forming the sides of the tomb, with a massive stone slab acting as the roof. This arrangement creates an enclosed space that would have served as a final resting place for the deceased. The precision with which these stones were placed speaks to the skills of the builders, who had a deep understanding of both functionality and aesthetics.

One notable feature of the tomb is its orientation, which may have been deliberately chosen to align with celestial events, a common theme in dolmen construction. This connection to the cosmos suggests that the builders not only placed importance on the physical act of burial but also on the spiritual implications of their location.
