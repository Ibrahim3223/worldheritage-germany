## History and Significance

Le Corbusier, born Charles-Édouard Jeanneret, was a Swiss-French architect whose career flourished in the early to mid-20th century. His architectural philosophy was grounded in the belief that buildings should enhance the quality of life for their inhabitants. He sought to redefine urban spaces, proposing designs that emphasized light, open space, and integration with nature. His ideas on the "Five Points of a New Architecture"—pilotis (supports), flat roofs, open floor plans, horizontal windows, and free façades—revolutionized the architectural landscape.

The sites that comprise the Architectural Work of Le Corbusier include numerous residential units, community buildings, and urban planning projects. Each structure reflects Le Corbusier's vision of a functional, efficient, and aesthetically pleasing environment. Among the notable examples are the Unité d'Habitation in Marseille, the Chapel of Notre Dame du Haut in Ronchamp, and the Villa Savoye.

Le Corbusier's work is not just an architectural achievement; it represents a broader cultural movement towards modernism. His concepts of urban living shaped not only the physical structures of cities but also their social fabric, encouraging communal living and interaction. As a result, visiting these sites provides insight into the historical context of urban development and the evolution of architectural thought during the 20th century.

## Architecture and Features

The Architectural Work of Le Corbusier is characterized by its innovative use of materials and design principles that prioritize functionality without sacrificing beauty. His buildings often feature dramatic geometric forms, open spaces, and large windows that invite natural light and promote a sense of openness.

Notable features of his designs include the use of concrete, which allowed for greater structural flexibility and creativity. Many of his buildings showcase a modernist aesthetic with clean lines and minimalist ornamentation, emphasizing the idea that form should follow function. His signature pilotis elevate buildings above the ground, creating open spaces underneath that encourage interaction and accessibility.

The Unité d'Habitation, for example, is a multi-family housing complex that integrates residential living with communal spaces, including shops and recreational areas. This design exemplifies Le Corbusier's vision of a self-sufficient community where residents could thrive in a shared environment.

Another remarkable structure is the Chapel of Notre Dame du Haut, which features sweeping curves and a sculptural quality that contrasts with traditional church architecture. Its design not only serves a spiritual purpose but also engages with the surrounding landscape, creating a dialogue between the natural and built environments.

Each site within this collection embodies Le Corbusier's principles and philosophies, representing a synthesis of art, architecture, and urban planning that has influenced generations of architects and designers.
