### II. Equipment Recommendations (250 words)

#### Essential Gear

**Camera Body:**
Full-frame preferred (better in low light), but APS-C works fine. What matters: High ISO performance (minimum ISO 3200 usable), manual controls

**Lenses - The Holy Trinity:**

**Wide-Angle (14-24mm or 16-35mm):**
- Essential for interiors
- Captures full vaulted ceilings
- Shows scale and space
- **My pick:** 16-35mm f/4 (sharp, manageable size)

**Standard Zoom (24-70mm):**
- Versatile for exteriors
- Detail shots
- Altars and chapels
- **My pick:** 24-70mm f/2.8 (low-light capability)

**Telephoto (70-200mm):**
- Facade details
- Sculpture and ornament
- Compression effects outside
- **My pick:** 70-200mm f/4 (lighter than f/2.8)

#### Nice to Have

**Tripod:** Ideal but often prohibited. Ask first.
**Remote Release:** If tripod allowed
**Polarizer:** For exterior shots (reduces glare)
**Lens Cloth:** For quick cleaning

#### Budget Options

**Can't afford pro gear?**
- Kit 18-55mm lens works if you understand its limits
- Rent specialty lenses for trips (€30-50/day)
- Smartphone: Better than you think for well-lit situations

**What I Actually Carry:**
16-35mm f/4, 24-70mm f/2.8, minimal accessories. Gothic cathedrals involve lots of walking—pack light.

### III. Understanding Cathedral Light (350 words)

#### The Challenge

Gothic architecture creates extreme contrast: brilliant stained glass against stone that absorbs light. Your camera meter gets confused. Your eye compensates; your camera doesn't.

#### Best Times to Shoot

**Morning (7:00-10:00 AM):**

**Cologne Cathedral specifically:**
- East-facing windows illuminated by morning sun
- Soft warm light (late morning)
- Fewer tourists (cathedrals open 6:00 AM)
- Security guards haven't started enforcing rules strictly yet

**What to shoot:** Interior wide angles, stained glass backlit, atmospheric light beams

**My settings:** ISO 1600-3200, f/5.6-f/8, 1/60-1/125s

**Afternoon (2:00-5:00 PM):**

**West-facing window light:**
- Stained glass at peak illumination
- Colors most saturated
- Can shoot into light for dramatic effect

**What to shoot:** Close-ups of stained glass, detail shots, light beams

**My settings:** ISO 800-1600, f/8-f/11, 1/125-1/250s

**Blue Hour (after closing):**

**Exteriors only** (cathedrals close 7:00-8:00 PM):
- Building floodlights on
- Sky still has color (not black)
- Perfect balance of artificial and natural light

**What to shoot:** Exterior facades, night photography, city context

**My settings:** ISO 800-1600, f/8-f/11, 1-4 seconds (tripod essential)

**Avoid Midday:**
Harsh overhead sun outside, flat light inside. If stuck shooting midday, focus on details that don't depend on beautiful light.

### IV. Camera Settings (450 words)

#### Interior Photography

**My Standard Interior Setup:**

**Aperture: f/5.6 to f/8**
- **Why:** Enough depth of field for architecture
- f/2.8: Too shallow (front in focus, back blurry)
- f/11-f/16: Need too-slow shutter or too-high ISO
- f/5.6-f/8: Sweet spot for sharpness and light gathering

**Shutter Speed: 1/60s minimum**
- **Why:** Hand-hold limit (with image stabilization)
- Slower = camera shake = blurry images
- Cathedrals are dim—you'll be pushing this limit
- If light permits, shoot 1/125s for safety

**ISO: 1600-3200 (don't be afraid)**
- Modern cameras handle ISO 3200 beautifully
- Slight noise > motion blur from slow shutter
- Noise reducible in post; blur isn't
- Test your camera's ISO limit before trip

**White Balance: Auto or Custom**
- Auto works surprisingly well
- Mixed lighting (daylight through windows + interior tungsten) confuses WB
- Shoot RAW so you can adjust in post

**Metering Mode: Spot or Center-Weighted**
- Evaluative/Matrix gets confused by contrast
- Spot meter on mid-tone (stone column, not window or shadow)
- Check histogram—avoid blown highlights in windows

**Shooting Mode: Aperture Priority or Manual**
- Aperture priority: Set f/8, let camera choose shutter/ISO (auto ISO)
- Manual: Full control but slower to adjust
- I use Aperture Priority, then check histogram and adjust exposure compensation

#### Example Settings by Situation:

**Wide interior shot (nave looking toward altar):**
- 16mm, f/8, 1/60s, ISO 2500
- Expose for mid-tones, let windows blow out slightly (you can't capture full range)

**Stained glass close-up:**
- 70mm, f/8, 1/250s, ISO 800
- Expose for the glass, let surrounding stone go black
- Creates dramatic contrast

**Detail (sculpture, capital, etc):**
- 50mm, f/5.6, 1/125s, ISO 1600
- If enough light, stop down to f/8 for more sharpness

**Light beams through windows:**
- 24mm, f/8, 1/125s, ISO 3200
- Underexpose slightly (darkroom makes beams more visible)
- Shoot when dust/incense in air (makes beams visible)

#### Exterior Photography

**My Standard Exterior Setup:**

**Golden Hour (sunrise/sunset):**
- f/8-f/11 (sharpness + depth)
- 1/125-1/500s (usually enough light)
- ISO 100-400
- Polarizer to deepen sky

**Blue Hour (twilight):**
- f/8-f/11
- 1-4 seconds (tripod essential)
- ISO 800-1600 (keep it reasonable)
- 2-second timer to avoid camera shake

### V. Composition Techniques (600 words)

#### Technique #1: Emphasize Vertical Lines

**What:** Gothic cathedrals soar upward—emphasize that verticality

**How:**
- Shoot in portrait orientation
- Use wide-angle lens
- Position yourself centered in nave
- Tilt up to capture full height
- Keep vertical lines straight (correct distortion in post if needed)

**Example:** Cologne Cathedral nave, 16mm, centered, looking up toward vaulting

**Settings:** f/8, 1/60s, ISO 2500

**Common Mistake:** Tilting camera up too much = converging verticals (columns lean in). Either embrace it or fix in Lightroom's transform panel.

**[See this technique at Cologne Cathedral](/sites/cologne-cathedral/)**

---

#### Technique #2: Frame Within Frame

**What:** Use architectural elements to frame your subject

**How:**
- Shoot through doorways, arches, columns
- Creates depth and context
- Guides viewer's eye to focal point

**Example:** Altar framed by nave arches, side chapel framed by archway

**Settings:** f/8 (keep foreground and background sharp), expose for subject

---

#### Technique #3: Isolate Details

**What:** Cathedrals are overwhelming—sometimes less is more

**How:**
- Use telephoto lens (70-200mm)
- Focus on single element (capital, sculpture, window section)
- Shallow depth of field acceptable here (f/4-f/5.6)
- Fill frame with your subject

**Example:** Single stained glass panel, carved capital with foliage, gargoyle

**Why it works:** Shows craftsmanship, creates variety in photo series

---

#### Technique #4: Light and Shadow

**What:** Gothic architecture creates dramatic light/shadow interplay

**How:**
- Look for light shafts through windows
- Expose for highlights (let shadows go black)
- Shoot when sun angle creates beams
- Slight underexposure increases drama

**Example:** Light beam cutting through dim interior, half-lit pillar

**Settings:** Spot meter on bright area, underexpose 1-2 stops

---

#### Technique #5: Human Scale

**What:** Include people to show cathedral scale

**How:**
- Wait for visitor to walk into frame
- Position them to show size comparison
- Silhouettes work well
- Don't make people the subject—they show scale

**Example:** Tiny figure in vast nave, person gazing up at vaulting

**Ethics:** Respect privacy, don't photograph faces clearly without permission

---

#### Technique #6: Symmetry

**What:** Many Gothic elements are symmetrical—use it

**How:**
- Center your composition
- Level your camera carefully
- Shoot down center axis of nave
- Twin towers, matching windows, etc.

**Example:** Looking down nave to altar (perfect center), west facade (twin towers)

---

#### Technique #7: Leading Lines

**What:** Use architectural lines to lead eye through image

**How:**
- Ribbed vaulting creates natural lines
- Rows of columns lead to altar
- Floor patterns guide viewer
- Diagonal compositions (more dynamic than straight-on)

**Example:** Side aisle with repeating arches, vaulting ribs converging

---

#### Technique #8: Exterior Context

**What:** Show cathedral in urban setting

**How:**
- Include surrounding buildings, streets, plazas
- Shoot from distance with telephoto (compression)
- Reflections in modern glass buildings
- Aerial perspective (elevated viewpoints)

**Example:** Cologne Cathedral with Rhine bridge, cathedral towering over old town

**Best viewpoint for Cologne:** Hohenzollern Bridge

### VII. Post-Processing (200 words)

**My Workflow:**

1. **Exposure correction:** Cathedrals usually need +0.5 to +1 stop
2. **Highlights/Shadows:** Pull highlights down, lift shadows (slightly)
3. **White Balance:** Warm it up (tungsten interior = too blue)
4. **Transform:** Fix converging verticals, level horizons
5. **Clarity/Texture:** Subtle increase (shows detail)
6. **Dehaze:** Careful (too much looks fake)
7. **Sharpening:** Essential (lots of fine detail)

**Software:** Lightroom Classic (my choice), Capture One, or Luminar

**Avoid:** Over-HDR look (multiple cathedrals ruined by excessive processing)

**Goal:** Make it look like what your eye saw, not hyper-real

### VIII. Rules & Etiquette (150 words)

**Always:**
- Check if photography allowed
- Ask about tripod rules
- No flash (damages art, disturbs visitors)
- Respectful clothing
- Silent shutter mode
- Step aside for worshippers

**Usually Not Allowed:**
- Tripods (ask first)
- During services (wait until after)
- Commercial use without permission
- Drones (obviously)

**Donations:**
If photography free, consider donating. Cathedral maintenance is expensive.

### IX. FAQ (8 questions, 250 words)

**1. Can I use a tripod in German cathedrals?**

Most prohibit tripods without prior permission. Cologne Cathedral: no. Smaller cathedrals: sometimes OK if you ask. Modern cameras handle high ISO well—you rarely need a tripod.

**2. Is flash allowed?**

Never use flash in cathedrals. It's disrespectful, damages artwork over time, and ruins the atmosphere you're trying to capture.

**3. Can I sell cathedral photos?**

Personal/editorial use usually fine. Commercial requires permission from cathedral administration. Check specific rules.

**4. What if it's too dark?**

Raise ISO (3200 or higher), open aperture (f/4-f/5.6), slow shutter (1/30s with stabilization). Or visit when light is better.

**5. How do I avoid tourists in photos?**

Visit opening time (6:00-7:00 AM), use slow shutter to blur moving people, or wait patiently for clear moments.

**6. Can I use a smartphone?**

Yes! Modern smartphones have excellent low-light capability. Challenges: Limited wide-angle, less control, noise at high ISO. But absolutely usable.

**7. How do I photograph stained glass?**

Expose for the glass (underexpose overall), shoot when sun shines through, use spot metering, shoot in RAW.

**8. What's the best lens for cathedrals?**

16-35mm f/4 or similar wide-angle zoom. You need wide for interiors, but not ultra-wide (creates too much distortion).

### X. Conclusion (150 words)

German cathedrals reward patient photographers. The play of light through Gothic windows, the soaring vaulted spaces, the centuries of craftsmanship—these are subjects that never get old.

My advice: Visit multiple times, at different times of day. Morning light creates different opportunities than afternoon. Spend time just looking before you shoot. Understand the space, then capture it.
